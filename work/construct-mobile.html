Apollo Construct

Bringing better user experience to the Apollo Construct mobile app

Overview

The Apollo mobile app makes it easier than ever for construction teams to manage their projects. The Apollo mobile app consolidates important data across the entire Apollo suite, so teams can track progress in one convenient place and on-the-go.

My Contributions

Revamped the entire app completely right from the information architecture, to interactions and visuals. Conceptualised the "Cross project" dashboard view and "Quick Add" feature.
Identified opportunities and areas that were previously unnoticed such as accounting for sun-glare, background noise, low-internet etc

Product Designer, Lead
Katerra, Full Time
Dec 2018 - Present

With our users expecting to do an increasing number of things on the mobile app, inconsistencies started to creep in, in both the flow and information architecture. We had to craft a more accessible, understandable app that affords all of Construct’s existing features in a consistent, logical and coherent manner.

Over time, the demands of the Construct app have grown exponentially. What started as a tool for managing tasks, updating drawings and recording daily logs, must now meet the needs of on-site workers across cities, in different situations – responding to schedule update requests, conducting inspections during different phases of a project, raising safety observations or even recording the observed weather conditions. Construct was being used for a variety of things that were never imagined at its inception. We worked closely with our PMs and on-site teams both in India and the US, to design a better app.

Here's how we went about redesigning one feature specifically, observations.

Initial Research

Our users were hesitant to fill out forms on mobile. Users were finding it hard to do simple tasks such as creating and editing an observation. They found interacting with the app very taxing. In our research phase, our data was telling us that observations feature wasn't being explored. So, our goal was to make the process of filling out a form as easy as possible. We looked to avoid clutter and build on existing mental models of our users.

80%

Our users felt they were spending far too much time to create an observation

Our users were spending more than 7 seconds to simply understand the form while creating an observation and over 400 seconds to complete the process. They found it complex and unintuitive.

We heard users tell us that they were not finding particular fields / calls-to-action, when in fact they were present. Often they had to return to the form, since they missed certain details.

Perception of complexity

We realised our users were scanning instead of reading. The first thing users did when they saw the form was, estimate how long it would take to complete it. Perception was crucial, when they saw too many fields, they were quick to abandon the process.

Cognitive Load

The old design presented all the fields stacked one below the other. The lack of hierarchy and structure and an increasing number of fields, made the form difficult to comprehend. The mental and physical load on the user were too high.

Our users were hesitant to fill out forms on mobile. So, our goal was to make the process of filling out a form as easy as possible. We looked to avoid clutter and build on existing mental models of our users. We used a simple layout to make it look familiar and reduce the amount of learning.

90%

Managers and on-site engineers said they couldn't see observations that required their attention the most and quickly decide which ones to dive into.

In the old app a lot of information was buried, making it difficult to view the critical observations. Managers told us what information was valuable to them and when it is most useful to see them. They wanted to see Observation Title, Status, Due Date, Priority and Assigneé. On-site engineers wanted to see ID and type as well.

We wanted to provide all these data points in one place, to help users decide which observation to dive into. After a lot of chopping and changing, we eventually landed on a consolidated card structure with all these data points.

70%

The users were spending far too much time scrambling across the page from one detail to the other. They were struggling to make associations and found interacting with the app taxing.

In the old design, the details page lacked any structure and hierarchy. The elements were ill-ordered and frustrated users. Important data points lacked emphasis and related data points weren’t grouped. Users had to hunt for information (For example, the status field followed the location field and was buried in the form).

Our goal was simple. We wanted to craft a details page with a simple structure, that provided the right amount of information (too much data can overwhelm while too little can cause confusion). We wanted users to quickly understand what they see and find what they want in a glance. We wanted the page to resemble a conversation.

Being mindful of the user’s environment

The old app was arduous, confusing and not functional, owing to which it was hardly used. In the redesign, we were careful to track every nuance and detail. One of the things we did was, we considered the context of use. We knew that a lot of our users will be using the app outdoors.

1. On-site engineers were often at an arm’s distance from the device, often amidst noise and a lot happening in the background.

We iterated a lot to decide what information to present, which actions to highlight. We ensured that the text was comfortably legible at an arm’s distance. We increased the prominence for essential information

2. A lot of our users were switching from holding the phone with one hand to holding it in two hands without even realising.

This meant that the common practice of placing low-priority, less-frequented actions at the top left corner of the screen wouldn’t work. To tackle this, when the user was performing an important action, we used confirmation modals.

3. Accounting for sun glare, low light environments and fat fingers.

We ensured the text is legible at every size, icons were precise, large action buttons and used minimal adornments for aesthetics. We ensured sufficient color contrast between elements so that users can see and use the app. We moved unessential content to secondary pages, reducing both the information density & cognitive strain in the process. We were also careful to use accessibility-compliant color combinations.

After the redesign we saw,

  • A sharp decrease in the app uninstalls
  • More users were using the app than ever before. The MAUs increased by 70%.
  • People were starting to explore the app. There was an increase in visits and usage of features in the Apollo mobile app, that weren't being used previously.
  • New feature requests for mobile. With the new experience people realised how convenient the mobile app can be. From being hesitant and having reservations about the mobile app (owing to the complex workflows), they wanted to do a lot more on mobile, on the go. This was a big behavioural change. A change in both user mindset and perception. This was a huge win for us !!

Takeaways

  • “The more you know your user the better the design will be.” This quote sums up my experience rather nicely. I learnt that both information design is very interdisciplinary. I learnt that it always started with the user.
  • While working on this redesign, I tried to have a dialog with the data; Poking at it, looking at it from different perspectives with users. We tried a number of ways to visualise the information, until we stumbled upon one way that made sense and was most useful to the user.
  • Here are some questions we kept asking ourselves,
    What works visually?
    What works from a design perspective & usability standpoint?
    Which way tells a story?
    Which way is it most helpful to the user?
    Are we using people language?
