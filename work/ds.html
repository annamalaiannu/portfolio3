Katerra is a global technology leader that has started the journey to humanize its technology with a systematic design approach to develop coherent, powerful and enabling solutions that provide meaningful experiences to their customers. Katerra aims to help transform construction through technology—every process and every product.

And Apollo is a huge part of this vision. Apollo is a software platform and suite of applications designed to support building projects and teams from beginning to end. While building Apollo, the focus was primarily of tech and business goals of Katerra. Katerra found itself in need of a design overhaul. My job was to create a unified design language to modernize IBM's enterprise software.

With even the most complex domains of industries starting to embrace technology, Katerra was looking for new ways to take their digital offering to the next level, with an experience crafted around the user.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
How we got here
 

In 2018, I started working at Katerra Technologies, Katerra's software wing which was responsible for the tech.  I came onboard as a contractor, responsible for the product design of the Apollo mobile app. Katerra didn't have a full-time product design team. The Apollo team was in a phase of transition. It was a time when the product team was just starting to recognize the value of user experience, thanks to some early feedback from real users. Prior to this the team was focused on solutions that technically work and met Katerra’s business objectives. There was no product design team in place.

 

The designs we shipped for mobile proved to be a gentle introduction to user experience. Soon I got pulled into every product in the Katerra’s Apollo suite and became a full time member of Katerra. I was asked to provide quick incremental UX tweaks and make workflows simpler. Product teams starting to realise that the designs can be improved was an important step.

 
 
 
 
 
Breaking old habits formed in the absence of good design
 

Products in the Apollo suite were stuck in a 90’s theme that we desperately needed to get out of. A lot of the patterns and interactions were out of date. The entire architecture was tangled, navigating across apps was unnecessarily complex. Workflows didn't function the way our users expected. Soon it was quite evident that we needed a complete revamp.
There was no brand identity, nothing uniquely Apollo - no defined color palette, typography, consistent iconography etc. Developers looked to reuse existing elements and widgets wherever possible, leaving product designers completely out of the picture. We had to craft a product agnostic design language which was cohesive and uniquely Apollo.

 
 


Apollo 1.0 was plagued with inconsistency and stuck in a 90's theme. Features were built as one-off solutions without a common language. This was largely due to time constraints, the existence of multiple teams that worked independently and multiple functional roles with different skillsets and perspectives.

 
 
 
 
 
Getting our feet wet: Building a Minimum
Viable Design System
 

We knew the enormity of the task at hand. It’s impossible to create a great design from the first attempt. In December 2019, we kicked off our first real effort to align product design and standardise components across products. We decided to build an MVP of some sort, a minimum viable design system.  We had a small multidisciplinary team (2 designers, 1 PO, 1 PM & 1 VP).

Since the team never really paid attention to design issues in the past, there were some glaring issues that we could easily fix. We identified base components such as data tables, input boxes, calls-to-action etc and formalised how these components looked and behaved. Out of these components we built a base component library and tested it out on Katerra's budgeting software. We had team members gather together on informal design reviews to discuss the pros and cons of individual solutions. This was a huge effort in itself. This effort also helped us better estimate the effort a complete revamp would take and how to go about it.

 
 

This step was crucial for us to get organizational buy-in. It taught us the basics and strategy - to gather and document opinions, reach out to a diverse team and collect as many opinions as early as possible, and have senior members at the right moments to get feedback when it mattered the most. We changed our weapon of choice as well, from Sketch to Figma. Creating the components in Sketch proved to be mess and a challenge to maintain.

 
 
 
 
 
 
Being Strategic
 

A huge chunk of the problems we had boiled down to the 3 categories. For each of these key problems, we worked to identify the source of the problem, get end user’s inputs and then use that information to design a solution.

 
 
 
     
  • Audits - We did a painstaking audit and recategorization of all our existing content because we noticed that users were often in stuck in certain workflows for ages, unsure where to go.

  • Strategic Pulse Checks with POs and Managers- Are we heading in the right direction? How do we prioritize explorations alongside product needs? What about scaling components to a global audience? How do we track our milestones? How do we involve others and build a culture of contribution?

Review often, review early. Having a constant feedback loop of actionable insights flowing in was invaluable to us. It gave a different perspective to look at things and to course correct early.

 
 
 
 
 
Establishing Design Principles
 

We wanted to unify our team around what’s most important to us and our users, and ensure consistency in decision making across designers and teams. We used our established design principles as a guide, at every stage of the design process and to refine the product’s look and feel. The principles are the foundation to the solutions we deliver. After a lively debate

 
 
 

1. Carefully Crafted

 

Before we decide to do anything, we must consider its usefulness and utility to others. Then, we must determine how committed we are to enthusiastically explore and progressively deliver the full potential of a design.

 
 
 

2. Cohesive & Unified

 

Successful systems require both fixed and fluid elements of expression. There must be no isolated units and random visual elements. The elements across Apollo must have a unified look, feel and tone. The innovative ways in which we use these elements delivers distinction and uniqueness.

 
 
 

3. Uniquely Apollo

 

Express our identity in-product in immediately recognizable ways. Make our products easier to use by humanizing the experience.

 
 
 

4. Progressive

 

Future proof

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Getting the "Baseline" right
 

We extended the new Apollo style guide to craft what we called the "Baseline". The baseline consists of our typography, colors, product logos, UI iconography, spacing, grid and navigation. This foundation proved essential for guiding our work in a unified direction while allowing room for us to individually explore creative design solutions.

These are the elements that make Apollo look like Apollo. Using these components is the minimum bar for every product in the Apollo suite. This was the part we built first and used it to communicate it to the rest of the teams.

 
 
 
 
Building Localised Subsystems
 

In Apollo, while each product is inextricably woven to the other, each product has it's own personality, it's own function. So it only made sense that we crafted the product specific components in the same manner.

The intent was to create components that can evolve. We didn't want to have a set of rigid rules and styles that all components had to adhere to. The subsystems are maintained by product teams who refer to it to guide design decisions or code implementation.

 


Each product on the Apollo suite has it's own subsystem. The subsystems are maintained by product teams who refer to it to guide design decisions or code implementation.

 
 
 
 
 
Apollo Web
 

Next up, is Apollo Web. The local design systems for each of our web based products come under this roof. It offers all the components found in a typical web design systems: buttons, dialogs, forms controls, and our local product subsystem. These components can be used in anything that’s built using web tech.

Apollo Web has the shared resources for our web-based platforms, but it also includes everything from the Baseline. All the components follow the patterns and guidelines defined in the Baseline. So the systems aren’t stand-alone things: they’re connected.

 
 
 
 
Apollo Mobile
 

Finally, we move to Apollo mobile. Apollo mobile is the place for common components that are shared across our mobile apps. At the moment, we are putting the pieces together for this one.
